Overview

My role in this project

Lucky Pig was a cross-functional group project where I wore two hats: designing the mobile UI in Figma and implementing the frontend in .NET MAUI with C#. This project pushed me creatively because casino design has its own visual language — high contrast, rich textures, a sense of luxury and excitement — that I had to learn and execute within MAUI's component system.

Beyond aesthetics, I was responsible for the app's game interaction design: how players would feel winning, how to surface their progress, and how to make every interaction feel rewarding. That meant designing gamification systems, not just screens.

01 · Research & Define

The design brief

"Build a casino mobile app that's engaging enough to keep users coming back — through game variety, rewards, and a visual experience that feels premium on a phone screen."

The challenge with casino UX is that you're fighting for attention in a space where every competitor uses the same vocabulary: neon lights, spinning reels, loud colours. My goal was to create something that felt luxurious and polished rather than garish — a casino you'd actually want to spend time in.

I spent time studying the UX patterns of successful gambling apps — how they handle session flow, how they surface wins, and critically, how they handle responsible gambling features without breaking the experience.

Layered Engagement

Research showed that successful casino apps retain users through layered rewards — immediate feedback (win animations), short-term goals (daily spin), and long-term progression (leaderboard rank). I designed all three layers.

MAUI Design Constraints

.NET MAUI had specific constraints: no CSS gradients by default, limited animation APIs, and platform-specific rendering differences between Android and iOS. My designs had to be achievable within these limits.

Responsible Design

Gambling apps have an ethical dimension. I designed session time displays and deposit limits to be accessible but non-intrusive — present without being preachy, because they needed to actually get used.

03 · Visual Design

The Lucky Pig visual identity

I made a deliberate choice to go dark and premium rather than neon-garish. The palette draws from high-end physical casinos — deep navy, velvet purple, and gold accents. This positioned Lucky Pig as a sophisticated experience rather than a cheap mobile slot machine.

03 · Design Decisions

Five choices that shaped Lucky Pig

1
Onboarding

Animated onboarding over a static welcome screen

First impressions matter immensely in entertainment apps. I designed a multi-step animated onboarding that introduced the mascot, explained coins and chips, and previewed the game lobby — all before asking for registration details. This built excitement before commitment.

2
Game UX

Distinct visual identity for each game

Rather than a single dark theme across all five games, I gave each game its own accent colour and table texture while staying within the core palette. Slots felt electric, Blackjack felt premium, Russian Roulette felt tense. Players navigated by feel, not just by title.

3
Gamification

Progress visibility at every screen, not just the profile

Most apps bury achievement progress in a profile tab — players forget it exists. I surfaced level progress, current streak, and coin balance in the persistent navigation bar so users always knew how close they were to their next reward. This drove re-engagement between sessions.

4
AI Integration

Gemini dealer as an in-game tutorial, not a separate feature

We integrated Gemini AI as a virtual dealer. Rather than a separate "Help" section, I embedded the dealer contextually within game screens — appearing when a player hesitated or hit an unusual hand in Blackjack. This felt natural, not intrusive.

5
Feedback

Win animations designed for emotional impact

I prototyped three different win animation styles before settling on a gold coin particle effect with a brief scale punch on the win amount. The key was timing: too short felt anticlimactic, too long felt disruptive. We settled on 800ms — just enough to feel rewarding without interrupting momentum.

Gamification Design

Keeping players engaged

Four distinct systems layered together to drive engagement at different timescales — from immediate (win animations) to long-term (leaderboard rank). Designing these as a system, not individual features, was key to making them feel coherent.

Achievement System

Unlockable badges for milestones — first win, 10 games played, reaching Gold tier

Leaderboard

Weekly rankings with tier icons. Players compete for prestige, not just coins

Daily Spin

Free daily reward wheel — creates a return habit outside of gambling sessions

Streak Multiplier

Consecutive win bonuses that compound — rewards skilled play and risk-taking

Problem → Solution

Key design challenges

Challenge

Dark UI is hard to keep accessible

Deep navy and purple backgrounds made it easy to accidentally place text with insufficient contrast — especially with gold text on purple tints.

Solution

Strict contrast checks in Figma

I added contrast checking to my design process and settled on a minimum 5:1 ratio for all body text. Gold on navy consistently hit 6.5:1.

Challenge

MAUI doesn't support CSS-style gradients natively

My Figma designs relied heavily on layered gradients — not available out of the box in XAML without custom renderers.

Solution

Custom gradient frames via overlapping elements

I recreated gradients using overlapping transparent BoxViews and LinearGradientBrush — matching my designs closely without third-party libraries.

Challenge

Russian Roulette had no visual tension

Early designs of the game looked like any other — the high-stakes feel wasn't coming through in the UI.

Solution

Red accent and countdown timer

I introduced a red-dominant colour theme unique to that game and a visible countdown that builds tension as the "chamber" loads — players reported it felt genuinely tense.

05 · Reflect

What Lucky Pig taught me

Key takeaways

  • Entertainment UX is emotion-first — every design decision must ask "how does this feel?" not just "does this work?"
  • MAUI frontend work sharpened my ability to translate visual designs into a constrained framework I hadn't used before
  • Gamification is most effective when it feels intrinsic — rewards should feel earned, not handed out
  • Dark UIs require more systematic thinking about contrast — I now check ratios at every step

What I'd do differently

  • Prototype key micro-interactions (win animations, card flips) earlier — we finalised these too late in the process
  • Conduct usability testing with players who don't know the rules — familiarity blinded us to confusing moments
  • Design a consistent motion language for the whole app, not per-screen animations
  • Explore haptic feedback design — mobile casino apps live or die by touch feel, not just visuals
